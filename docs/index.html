What is the Challenge Recipe?

As part of Web Design and Technologies, you’ve been asked to create a web based program that helps students of design programming become better programmers. The Challenge Recipe is a guide for how to do that.

But this isn’t a mock brief, we’re going to choose the best submissions from this assessment and use them to teach design programming students! So do your best and enjoy making your challenge.

Your learning goal and puzzle trick

All good challenges are puzzles. As such, we’ll use these terms interchangeably from here on in!
Puzzles give us a sense of play, imply a sense of control over an environment and focus our attention to solve a logical problem.

Our puzzles will need two things to get started: your learning goal and your puzzle trick.

Definitions

Learning goal What you want the user to learn by solving the puzzle.
Puzzle Trick The logical problem users need to figure out to solve a logical problem. e.g. the algorithms to solve a rubix cube depending on the current configuration.

Below is an example learning goal and puzzle trick for a challenge on Transforming Shapes.
You can find the example challenge here for reference.

Examples

Learning goal Understand how translate and rotate affect the canvas and how shapes are drawn.
Puzzle Trick Together rotate() and translate() position and orientate the canvas to any part of the screen. You draw one blade of the windmill, set the origin to the middle of the screen and then use rotate() to iterate the canvas orientation every time a for loop runs

Your Puzzle Sketch

Human centredness asserts firstly, that we must always put people before machines, however complex or elegant that machine might be, and, secondly, it marvels and delights at the ability and ingenuity of human beings.
- Mike Cooley 2008

With your learning goal and puzzle trick, think about your puzzle from the perspective of the design programming student. An easy way to do this is to make a puzzle for yourself back when you were design programming! This is a form of human centred design and is a useful perspective for designing throughout design computing and beyond.

From this perspective, it’s useful to sketch out the ideal visualisation for your puzzle to match your learning goal and puzzle trick.

You’re figuring out you want the complete puzzle to look like.

When visualising your puzzle, consider patterns in nature, artisitcs influences or code projects on YouTube or GitHub as inspiration. To check if you’re on the right track think about it in the terms that Scott puts forward; is there a clear problem to solve and is it fun?

Break your puzzle into 'puzzle pieces'

So you’ve just created your puzzle from scratch as the fantastic puzzle designer you are! Now you need to break it up into pieces for the user to put back together!

This is where the user learns something and has fun along the way.

You need to figure out how to break the puzzle into pieces in a way that’s not too hard to understand but not too easy that it doesn’t require focus to solve the puzzle.

Think what kind of puzzle pieces you’d want to start with if you were doing the challenge as a design programming student.

Jumble your puzzle

Now you’ve figured out what your puzzle pieces are, it’s time to put them in a messy configuration that the user has to put together via the puzzle trick to reach their learning goal!

An example of this might be jumbling up a rubix cube before giving it to a friend to solve.

Think about how you’d want the puzzle laid out for you so that you’d learn something when you’ve solved it.
